Mapping the Roots of Hunger in Ontario



To understand hunger patterns in Ontario better, this project sought to reveal not only where Ontario’s hunger needs exist but also their trends in relation to other factors. Intersections between food bank use and socio-economic variables of interest at regional and local scales reveal an array of geographic relationships and patterns previously invisible.


This project supports the research and advocacy efforts of FeedOntario, a non-profit organization working to unite and support local food banks through a vision of ending hunger and poverty across Ontario.

About Our Methodology

Stage 1: Data Acquisition and Pre-Processing

To start, we first acquired food bank intake data from our client, FeedOntario, though their data management partner, Link2Feed. The acquired data was captured from the period of January 1 to December 31, 2018. Next, we retrieved census geographic boundary files from Statistics Canada (2016), as well as an electoral riding geographic boundary file from Elections Ontario (2018). Socioeconomic/socio-demographic datasets were also retrieved from Statistics Canada from the Census Profile (2016). We also obtained a Postal Code Conversion File (PCCF) from the Fleming College Library to facilitate geocoding.

For pre-processing, we utilized the Pandas Python module to effectively combine these large datasets and reduce them to only those census profile characteristics we were most interested in. We also worked to organize and clean the food bank intake data provided by Link2Feed to ensure accuracy and usability for subsequent processes and applications. The Postal Code Conversion File (PCCF) enabled the geocoding of the Link2Feed dataset so that it could be mapped.


Stage 2: Database Design and/or Processing

We created two databases, one with data attributed to Ontario’s electoral ridings and the other to a custom “mixed census” study area that introduced a more fine-grained local level of analysis across Ontario census subdivisions and census tracts. Each database included food bank use data and census variables of interest which were spatially joined to areas within the defined geographic boundaries.

Stage 3: Spatial and Statistical Analysis

We imported our datasets into an RStudio project using R-ArcGIS Bridge to test bivariate correlations between total food bank clients per capita and various census characteristics of interest through Spearman's Rank correlation coefficient. Simple scatter plots generated in R confirmed monotonic relationships in our data that demonstrated statistical significance. Census variables rendered in the subsequent cartographic visualization and presentation component were deemed statistically significant in R when plotted against food bank clients per capita.

Stage 4: Cartographic Visualization and Presentation

Our client’s primary goal was to intersect variables of interest to see how they compare and contrast and to visualize these relationships across particular geographies. With this in mind, we adopted a bivariate choropleth mapping technique and classified our data using the quantile method. This classification method ranked all datasets into quantitatively low, medium, or high categories with consideration for how the data was distributed within each dataset. The bivariate technique facilitated the crossing of variables such that the map reflected the relative extent of “agreement” or “disagreement” between variables.

For example, a local area that exhibits high food bank use as well as a high proportion of rented households would be considered a high level of “agreement,” whereas with a scenario of high food bank use and a low proportion of rented households, the opposite would be true.


Stage 5: Building the "Final Product"

A series of bivariate maps were configured in ArcGIS Online and subsequently integrated into ArcGIS Dashboards to provide further context, summary statistics, local area pop-ups and additional location-based information for each map (or variable pair). Finally, these dashboards were consolidated into a Story Map Collection which provides links to our configured apps, a demo, as well as some additional context about the project.

About Our Client

Founded in 1992, FeedOntario (formerly known as the Ontario Association of Food Banks) is a multi-service agency that unites food banks and local communities across Ontario through a vision of ending hunger and poverty. FeedOntario works hard to ensure Ontario's food banks are properly equipped with the necesssary food supplies to serve their local communities. They also promote change through extensive research and advocacy efforts concerning hunger needs and trends across the province and showcase their findings in an annual hunger report.
